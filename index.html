The Fighter

Boxing Career

From a 12-year-old who took up boxing after his bicycle was stolen, to the most celebrated heavyweight champion in history.

1954

A Champion is Born

12-year-old Cassius Clay began training in Louisville, Kentucky, after his bicycle was stolen. Police officer and boxing trainer Joe Martin pointed the angry boy toward the gym instead of the streets.

1960

Olympic Gold

At just 18, Clay traveled to Rome and won the gold medal in the light heavyweight division. Upon returning home, he was refused service at a Louisville diner — while wearing his medal.

1964

Shocking the World

As a 7-to-1 underdog, the 22-year-old Clay defeated Sonny Liston for the heavyweight title. He shouted to reporters: "I shook up the world!" Two days later, he announced his conversion to Islam and took the name Muhammad Ali.

1967

The Exile

Ali refused induction into the U.S. Army during the Vietnam War, declaring "I ain't got no quarrel with them Viet Cong." He was stripped of his title, banned from boxing for three years, and faced a five-year prison sentence. He sacrificed the prime of his career for his principles.

1971

Fight of the Century

Ali returned to face undefeated Joe Frazier at Madison Square Garden in one of the most anticipated fights in history. Both fighters were guaranteed $2.5 million. Frazier won by unanimous decision — Ali's first professional loss.

1974

The Rumble in the Jungle

In Kinshasa, Zaire, Ali deployed his famous "rope-a-dope" strategy against the fearsome George Foreman, who entered the fight 40-0 with 37 knockouts. Ali allowed Foreman to tire himself out, then knocked him out in the eighth round to reclaim the heavyweight title at age 32.

1975

Thrilla in Manila

The third and final fight between Ali and Frazier was so brutal that Ali said afterward, "It was the closest thing to dying that I know of." Frazier's corner stopped the fight after 14 rounds in over 100°F heat. Ali was too exhausted to celebrate.

1978

Three-Time Champion

After losing the title to Leon Spinks, Ali won the rematch seven months later in front of 63,350 fans at the New Orleans Superdome — becoming the first boxer in history to win the world heavyweight championship three separate times.

Beyond The Ring

Legacy & Activism

Ali transcended boxing to become one of the most influential figures of the 20th century.

A Voice for the Voiceless

Muhammad Ali used his platform to fight for racial justice, religious freedom, and human dignity at a time when doing so could cost you everything. His refusal to serve in Vietnam, embrace of Islam, and outspoken Black pride made him a polarizing figure — and then an American hero.

Civil Rights Pioneer

Ali's Supreme Court victory in 1971 overturned his draft conviction unanimously, vindicating his stance as a conscientious objector. A generation of Black Americans watched him declare his own beauty and greatness and found the courage to do the same.

Global Humanitarian

Ali traveled to Iraq in 1990 to negotiate the release of 15 American hostages. He visited hospitals, schools, and villages across Africa, Asia, and the Middle East, always believing fame was a tool for helping others.

Religious Freedom

Joining the Nation of Islam in 1964 and later Sunni Islam, Ali sacrificed millions in endorsements and faced intense backlash. He became a global symbol of the right to practice one's faith freely and without persecution.

Cultural Transformer

Ali's wit, poetry, and showmanship changed how athletes interact with media forever. He coined the trash-talk genre, predicted fight rounds, and proved that personality could be as powerful as punches.

Parkinson's Advocate

Diagnosed in 1984, Ali became the most famous face of Parkinson's disease. His lighting of the Olympic cauldron at the 1996 Atlanta Games — hands trembling but spirit unbroken — remains one of sport's most powerful images.

Presidential Honor

In 2005, Ali received the Presidential Medal of Freedom from George W. Bush — the nation's highest civilian honor, recognizing a lifetime of service and courage both inside and outside the ring.

Did You Know?

01

Against Cleveland Williams in 1966, Ali landed over 100 punches, scored 4 knockdowns, and was hit only 3 times in the entire fight.

02

Ali was undefeated in his first 31 professional fights before losing to Joe Frazier in 1971's "Fight of the Century" at Madison Square Garden.

03

Born Cassius Marcellus Clay Jr. on January 17, 1942, he was named after a 19th-century Kentucky abolitionist and politician.

04

The "Thrilla in Manila" against Frazier was fought in over 100°F heat. Ali said it was "the closest thing to dying" he'd ever experienced.

05

Ali's iconic "rope-a-dope" against George Foreman was improvised. His corner didn't know the plan — Ali just leaned back on the ropes and let Foreman punch himself out.

06

At the 1960 Rome Olympics, 18-year-old Ali was so afraid of flying that he bought a parachute from an Army surplus store and wore it on the plane.
